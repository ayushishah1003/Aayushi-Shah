Intermolecular Forces
Forces acting between molecules — weaker than covalent bonds, but they govern boiling point, melting point, viscosity, and solubility. Each animation below shows electron cloud movement at the molecular scale.
⚡ London Dispersion Forces (LDF)
All atoms & molecules · Instantaneous dipole → induced dipole
  • Electron clouds constantly fluctuate, creating a momentary δ+/δ− on each atom
  • This instantaneous dipole induces an opposite dipole in the neighbour
  • Strength ↑ with more electrons, higher molar mass, and greater surface area
  • Branching reduces surface area → weaker LDF (e.g. neopentane vs pentane)
HeArI₂CH₄C₆₀C₈H₁₈
🔗 Permanent Dipole–Dipole
Polar molecules only · Requires net molecular dipole ≠ 0
  • Unequal sharing of electrons creates a permanent δ+ and δ− end
  • Requires polar bonds AND asymmetric geometry (dipoles must not cancel)
  • Molecules align δ+ of one toward δ− of the next
  • Stronger than LDF for similar-sized molecules
HClSO₂H₂SCHCl₃PCl₃
💧 Hydrogen Bonding
H bonded to N, O, or F only · Strongest intermolecular force
  • H bonded to N, O, or F becomes very strongly δ+ (small, electronegative atoms)
  • δ+ H is attracted to a lone pair on N, O, or F of a neighbouring molecule
  • Water has 4 H-bond sites (2 donors + 2 acceptors) → exceptionally high BP
  • Explains anomalous properties: high BP of H₂O, ice less dense than water

🔷 Network Covalent Bonding
Giant lattice · No discrete molecules exist
  • Every atom is covalently bonded to all neighbours in one continuous network
  • The entire solid = one giant macromolecule (no intermolecular forces)
  • Melting requires breaking many covalent bonds → extremely high MP/BP
  • Hard and insoluble; poor conductors (except graphite)

⚡ Delocalised π Electrons (Graphite)
σ bonds in-layer · VdW between layers · π enables conduction
  • Each C makes 3 σ bonds (trigonal planar, 120°) in a flat hexagonal layer
  • 4th electron joins a delocalised π cloud above/below the layer → conducts electricity
  • Layers held together by weak London dispersion forces only → soft, lubricant
  • Layers can slide over each other easily → marks paper, used in pencils

📊 Relative Strength
Approximate energy ranges — varies with molecule size
  • Network covalent > H-bonding > Dipole–dipole > London dispersion
  • Large non-polar molecules (e.g. I₂, C₈H₁₈) can have stronger LDF than small polar ones
  • Key rule: more electrons + larger surface area = stronger LDF
  • Higher IMF → higher boiling point, higher viscosity, lower vapour pressure

Carbon Allotrope Comparison
💎 Diamond
  • Giant covalent 3D network — one macromolecule
  • Hardest natural substance (Mohs 10)
  • Electrical insulator — no free electrons
  • Tetrahedral C atoms, all σ bonds, 109.5°
  • BP: 3550°C — all bonds must break to melt
✏️ Graphite
  • Giant covalent 2D layers — hexagonal sheets
  • Soft, marks paper — layers slide (VdW)
  • Electrical conductor — delocalised π electrons
  • Trigonal planar C, 120° angles, σ + π bonds
  • VdW between layers only (~335 pm spacing)
⚽ Buckminsterfullerene C₆₀
  • Molecular solid — discrete C₆₀ molecules
  • Soluble in non-polar solvents (like dissolves like)
  • Can superconduct when doped with K
  • 20 hexagonal + 12 pentagonal faces
  • Only LDF between molecules → lower MP ~600°C
